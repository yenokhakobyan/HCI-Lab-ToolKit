Understanding Energy Units ‚ö°

What's a Kilowatt-Hour?

When you pay your electricity bill, you're paying for kilowatt-hours (kWh) ‚Äî the standard unit of energy consumption. Think of it this way: if you use 1,000 watts of power continuously for one hour, you've consumed exactly 1 kWh.

The Formula: kWh = Watts √ó Hours √∑ 1,000

How Much Do Appliances Use?

Every appliance has a wattage rating ‚Äî the power it draws when running:

  • LED bulb: 10W (very efficient)
  • Laptop: 50W (low power)
  • Refrigerator: 150W (runs constantly)
  • Air conditioner: 1,500W (high draw)
  • Electric heater: 2,000W (highest draw)
Try it: 1,500W AC √ó 4 hours √∑ 1,000 = 6 kWh

Introduction to Green Energy üåç

The Shift to Clean Energy

Renewable energy comes from natural sources that continuously replenish themselves. Unlike coal, oil, and natural gas ‚Äî which take millions of years to form and release harmful CO2 when burned ‚Äî renewables offer a sustainable path forward.

Four Major Types

Each renewable source has specific location requirements:

  • Solar: Requires 5+ peak sun hours daily. Panels convert sunlight directly to electricity. Reduces home CO2 by 80%.
  • Wind: Needs flat, open terrain for consistent airflow. Turbines spin generators. Reduces CO2 by 85%.
  • Hydro: Requires a flowing river to spin turbines. Very reliable but geographically limited. Reduces CO2 by 75%.
  • Geothermal: Taps underground heat near volcanic zones. Works 24/7 regardless of weather.

Why It Matters

The average home using fossil fuel electricity produces 7.5 tons of CO2 annually. Switching to renewables can dramatically cut this footprint.

To calculate savings: 7.5 tons √ó reduction % = tons saved

Solar Panel Efficiency ‚òÄÔ∏è

How Solar Panels Work

Solar panels contain photovoltaic cells that convert sunlight into electricity. When photons from sunlight hit these cells, they knock electrons loose, creating an electrical current. The efficiency rating tells you what percentage of sunlight actually becomes usable electricity.

Choosing Your Panel Type

Higher efficiency means more power from the same roof space:

  • Monocrystalline: 20% efficiency ‚Äî premium choice, best for limited roof space
  • Polycrystalline: 16% efficiency ‚Äî solid mid-range, good value balance
  • Thin-Film: 11% efficiency ‚Äî budget option, requires more area

Location Matters: Peak Sun Hours

Not all sunlight is equal. Peak sun hours measure the equivalent hours of full-intensity sunlight your location receives daily:

  • Phoenix: 6.5 hrs ¬∑ Denver: 5.0 hrs
  • Chicago: 4.0 hrs ¬∑ Seattle: 3.5 hrs

Battery Storage üîã

Why Add Battery Storage?

Solar panels generate electricity only during daylight, but your home needs power around the clock. Battery storage captures excess daytime energy so you can use it at night or during outages ‚Äî making your solar system truly independent.

Understanding Battery Chemistry

Usable capacity is what matters ‚Äî batteries shouldn't be fully drained, so you can only use a portion:

  • Lithium-Ion (Li-Ion): Use 80% of rated capacity. Lasts ~10 years. Most common technology.
  • LFP (Lithium Iron Phosphate): Use 90% of rated capacity. Lasts ~15 years. Premium longevity.
  • Lead-Acid: Use only 50% of rated capacity. Lasts ~4 years. Cheapest upfront but poor long-term value.

Products on the Market

  • Tesla Powerwall: 13.5 kWh rated, Li-Ion chemistry, $9,000
  • LG Chem RESU: 10 kWh rated, Li-Ion chemistry, $6,500
  • Enphase IQ: 5 kWh rated, LFP chemistry, $4,500

The True Cost of Going Solar

A typical 6kW residential system costs around $18,000 before incentives. However, the federal government offers a generous 30% tax credit, reducing your actual out-of-pocket cost to just $12,600.

Calculating Your Annual Savings

Your savings depend on two factors ‚Äî how much electricity your system produces and what you currently pay per kWh:

Annual Savings = kWh Produced √ó Your Rate

A well-positioned 6kW system typically generates 7,500 to 9,000 kWh per year, depending on your location's sunlight.

The Payback Period

This tells you how many years until your savings equal your investment:

Payback Years = Net Cost √∑ Annual Savings

Choosing a Plan üéØ

Understanding Your Options

When going solar, you'll choose between different system sizes, financing methods, and whether to include battery backup. Each choice involves trade-offs between upfront cost, long-term profit, and features.

Five Common Paths

  • Plan A (4kW System): $5,600 upfront. Smaller system, lower output. 25-year profit: +$15K. You own it.
  • Plan B (6kW System): $12,600 upfront. Standard home size. 25-year profit: +$29K. You own it.
  • Plan C (6kW + Battery): $21,000 upfront. Includes backup power for outages. 25-year profit: +$22K. You own it.
  • Plan D (Financed 6kW): $0 down, pay $150/month for 10 years. 25-year profit: +$24K. You own it after loan payoff.
  • Plan E (Solar Lease): $0 down, pay $95/month forever. 25-year profit: +$8K. You never own the system.
Key insight: Ownership (A-D) builds equity. Leasing (E) means lower commitment but also lower returns and no asset ownership.
